<p class="lede">In 1992, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ward_Cunningham" target="_blank">Ward Cunningham</a> <a href="https://cmdev.com/papers/debt-metaphor/" target="_blank">introduced</a> the idea of <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Technical_debt" target="_blank">technical debt</a> to explain a refactoring decision to his boss: code written to reflect your current understanding of a problem carries an implicit debt, because that understanding will change. You ship what you know, learn from the release, then pay the debt back by refactoring to match what you've learned since.</p>

The term escapes. Within a decade, a metaphor about unfinished thinking had become a licence for unfinished code. Cunningham was describing something specific: the inevitable gap between what you've learned and what your code reflects, widening every day and compounding like interest.

<span class="pullquote" text-content="Epistemic debt lives in the understanding, not the code."></span>Cunningham's original insight deserves a better name. In 2019, a team of industrial engineers [coined the term](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-030-20040-4_8) epistemic debt to describe what they were seeing on the factory floor: manufacturing systems that produced correct outputs without anyone understanding their internals, and the compounding cost of that ignorance. The name sounds academic, but the problem is mundane. Technical debt sounds like it lives in the code, which is why people reach for refactoring to fix it. Epistemic debt lives in the understanding, not the code, and you can rewrite every line without reducing it. Picture the file every organisation has: ten thousand lines of Python, a wiki page that says "do not touch" linking to a [post-mortem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmortem_documentation) from the time someone rebooted the server and the overnight batch failed. The operations desk spent their weekend recovering by hand. The last person who understood the file left three years ago, and what the team knows about why it works has decayed to folklore.

## The fence across the road

<span class="pullquote left" text-content="Every line of code is someone's fence."></span>[G.K. Chesterton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G._K._Chesterton) once [described](https://www.chesterton.org/taking-a-fence-down/) a fence standing across a road. A reformer sees no obvious purpose and proposes to tear it down, but Chesterton's response is that if you cannot explain why the fence was built, you are not the person to remove it. Every line of code is someone's fence, and legacy code in particular contains three kinds of behaviour that look identical from the outside. Intentional behaviour: the system does what it was designed to do. Accidental behaviour: bugs, race conditions, edge cases never anticipated. And the treacherous third kind: accidentally intentional behaviour, where a bug has been running in production long enough that other systems, processes and people now [depend on it](https://www.hyrumslaw.com/). If there were only two kinds, intended and unintended, the problem would be tractable: read the code, decide which is which. The third kind collapses that distinction. An accidentally intentional behaviour is both a bug and a feature simultaneously, and no amount of reading the code in isolation will tell you which label applies.

[Raymond Chen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raymond_Chen_(Microsoft)) has [catalogued](https://devblogs.microsoft.com/oldnewthing/20031224-00/?p=41363) this case by case across two decades of blog posts, each one a forensic reconstruction of some application's dependency on behaviour that was never promised. Across successive versions of [Windows](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microsoft_Windows), Microsoft discovered that popular applications [relied on undocumented behaviour](https://www.joelonsoftware.com/2004/06/13/how-microsoft-lost-the-api-war/) in earlier releases, including outright bugs, and rather than break those applications, the Windows team shipped [compatibility shims](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shim_(computing)) that detected specific programs and reproduced the old, broken behaviour. The bugs had become load-bearing. Remove them, and the ecosystem collapses.

[Michael Feathers'](https://michaelfeathers.silvrback.com/) answer in [Working Effectively with Legacy Code](https://www.oreilly.com/library/view/working-effectively-with/0131177052/) is to write [characterisation tests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Characterization_test) that pin the system's current behaviour, then refactor with confidence. This gives you a third place to express semantics, distinct from both the code and whatever documentation exists. But a characterisation test is a set of points: specific inputs mapped to observed outputs. It tells you what the system does here and here and here without giving you rules you can reason about in between. You can pin a thousand behaviours and still not know which are load-bearing, because the test suite doesn't tell you what the system is *for*. To answer that, you need a surface rather than a scatter plot: a representation that encodes general rules and constraints, something you can interrogate at coordinates you haven't sampled yet.

## Semantic triangulation

In the 1850s, the [Great Trigonometric Survey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Trigonometrical_Survey) of India measured the latitude difference between Kalianpur and Kaliana using two independent methods: [geodetic triangulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangulation_(surveying)) across a chain of stations, and astronomical observation using a plumb bob sighted against the stars. The two should have agreed but differed by 5.236 arc seconds. Everyone expected the Himalayas to deflect the plumb bob, but when [Archdeacon Pratt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Henry_Pratt) calculated the expected deflection from the mountains' visible mass, he got three times the observed discrepancy. The mountains were pulling only a third as hard as they should have been. The disagreement between methods didn't reveal an error in either. It revealed that the mountains have deep roots of lighter rock, floating like icebergs in denser mantle beneath, a structure invisible from the surface. The theory that emerged, [isostasy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isostasy), reshaped geology.

This is triangulation in its deepest sense. Two bearings on known points always give you exactly one intersection, a candidate position with no way to tell whether it's right. The geometry is too clean: a single crossing looks like confirmation even when one measurement is wrong. Add a third bearing and the clean intersection breaks apart. Instead of a point you get a triangle. The size and shape of that triangle won't tell you which bearing is off, but it tells you that something is, and gives you a region to investigate rather than a false certainty to build on.

<span class="pullquote" text-content="Three independent encodings of the same intent isn't redundancy. It's resilience."></span>A behavioural specification provides that third bearing on a codebase. Three copies of the same file is redundancy: protection against loss. Three independent encodings of the same intent is resilience. Code expresses intent as executable logic and tests capture it as expected outcomes. The spec encodes it differently: as structured rules and constraints. Each representation captures something the others miss, and their value lies in the pattern of their disagreements.

That intent is already embedded in any codebase, woven through its branches, state transitions and conditional logic, but it has never been written down as such. It's an implicit model that the system carries in its structure, recoverable only by reading every path with more patience than humans can sustain. Point an LLM at the codebase and you can extract that model as an explicit spec: a compressed, structured account of what the system appears to do and why, precise enough to interrogate. We use [Allium](https://juxt.github.io/allium) for this, a specification language we [built for exactly this kind of work](/blog/from-specification-to-stress-test).

With the spec in hand, you have three bearings instead of two, and where they diverge, you get a triangle rather than a point. The spec doesn't hand you a verdict. It hands you a landscape with a topology of its own. A rule of disproportionate complexity is a peak: the system is working around something, and the height of the workaround marks how much pressure that something exerts. Contradictions within the spec are fault lines where business logic evolved in incompatible directions, the ground pulling apart beneath the surface. Where the spec flatly contradicts the test assertions, you have the starkest feature: one of them is wrong, and the landscape won't tell you which. You still have to walk the terrain, but you can see it now. Through an iterative process of examining these features, the three kinds of behaviour start to separate: intentional, accidental and accidentally intentional.

We've run this on systems of many tens of thousands of lines, surfacing bugs alongside brittle code that hasn't broken yet and business logic that nobody could confidently call correct or incorrect.

## The newest legacy

Legacy code, by one useful definition, isn't defined by age: it's code where the intent behind it can no longer be explained. By that measure, a greenfield application shipped in an afternoon by an engineer working with an AI pair programmer qualifies the moment it's merged. The system runs, the tests pass (if there are tests), but the reasoning behind specific decisions was made by a model with [no memory of making them](https://failingfast.io/ai-epistemic-debt/). The old form of epistemic debt accumulated over years as teams turned over and institutional knowledge decayed. The new kind arrives on day one. A team with AI tooling can generate more functionality in a week than it can review in a month, and the debt compounds faster than anyone can track.

<span class="pullquote" text-content="The decisions were made, the code works, the tests pass, but the thinking behind each choice evaporated with the session."></span>The consensus, if you spend time in engineering circles, is that everyone *else* is being less rigorous. Stories of agents deleting production databases and overwriting email inboxes circulate with a mixture of horror and satisfaction, confirming that AI-assisted development is a race to the bottom. The comfortable move is to assume everyone with a Cursor tab open is vibing, and to feel quietly superior about it.

The reality is less tidy. Alongside the headline failures, there are engineers doing careful, considered work faster than was previously possible, applying the same rigour they always did with tools that let them cover more ground. The problem is that their reasoning leaves no trace. An AI pair programmer has no memory of the trade-offs it weighed, the alternatives it discarded or the constraints it respected. The decisions were made, the code works, the tests pass, but the *thinking* behind each choice evaporated with the session. A team that reviews the pull request sees the output, not the process, and has no way to distinguish thoughtful generation from careless acceptance.

This is epistemic debt arriving on day one. The spec becomes the most valuable artefact in the system: it is the thinking, written down in a form that survives the session, concrete enough to diff and challenge. For new systems it pulls double duty: diagnosing what's already been built and anchoring what comes next, keeping the model focused on purpose while giving it latitude on implementation.

## What the code remembers

Cunningham's debt was always about thinking. Not sloppy code, not missing tests, but the widening distance between what a system does and what anyone on the team believes it does. The file that no one dares touch and the application that shipped last Tuesday carry the same burden: decisions that no one can reconstruct, embedded in a structure that no one can read at the scale required.

The terrain is there whether you map it or not. Mountains have roots you cannot see from the surface, and the only way to discover them is to measure the same thing in more than one way and pay attention when the measurements disagree. Two bearings, two categories, two representations of intent: in each case, the pair seems sufficient until it isn't. The third is what shows you what you're standing on.
