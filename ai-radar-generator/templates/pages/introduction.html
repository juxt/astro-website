An introduction to the AI Radar from JUXT CTO, Henry Garner

Well, 2025 finally WAS the year of agents. After several years of bold predictions, agentic AI systems have genuinely begun delivering value in production environments. The gap between demo and deployment has narrowed considerably, though perhaps not as dramatically as the conference keynotes suggested. This quarter's radar reflects that maturation.

We've added Temporal to our Adopt ring, recognising that durable workflow orchestration has become essential infrastructure for agents that need to survive failures, handle human approvals, and run reliably over days rather than seconds. We're also seeing organisations move beyond prototypes to ask harder questions: how do we understand our processes before automating them? How do we monitor agents in production? The new entries for process mining and LLM observability reflect this shift from experimentation to operation.

At the same time there's the growing recognition that LLMs alone aren't sufficient for every problem. Neurosymbolic AI, combining neural networks with symbolic reasoning, has emerged as a practical architecture for systems requiring explainable decisions or guaranteed rule compliance. We've expanded our coverage of Prolog, added entries for phased ontology development and LinkML, and introduced neurosymbolic AI as a technique worth assessing. For regulated industries especially, the ability to couple an LLM's flexibility with auditable logical inference addresses limitations that prompt engineering alone cannot solve.

Finally, AI is increasingly escaping the screen. Foundation models purpose-built for robotics and physical systems are maturing rapidly, and digital twin platforms like NVIDIA Omniverse are enabling organisations to train and test AI in simulation before deployment. We've added coverage of both areas for teams working at the intersection of AI and the physical world.

Beyond these themes, one thing has become undeniable: AI is now a fundamental part of how we build software. From code generation and review to testing, documentation, and deployment, AI tools are touching every aspect of the software development lifecycle. What began as autocomplete suggestions has evolved into genuine collaboration between developers and AI systems.

As always, this radar represents our current thinking based on what we're seeing across client engagements. The landscape continues to shift quickly, and we expect our recommendations to evolve with it.

Henry Garner (CTO, JUXT), January 2026

Radar overview

Our radar visualises the AI landscape across two dimensions: quadrants divide the space by category, while radiants indicate our confidence in each technology.

Quadrants

The radar is divided into four quadrants, each representing a category of AI technology:

Techniques

Methodologies, architectural patterns, and practices for building AI systems: approaches like RAG, prompt engineering, agent design patterns, and evaluation methodologies. The "how" of AI development.

Languages & frameworks

Programming languages, libraries, and frameworks that form the building blocks of AI applications. From orchestration frameworks to specialised languages for symbolic reasoning, these are the tools you write code with.

Tools

Software that enhances AI development workflows without being embedded in your application code: IDE extensions, CLI utilities, testing frameworks, and observability solutions.

Platforms

Infrastructure and managed services that host and run AI workloads: cloud AI services, vector databases, model serving platforms, and MLOps infrastructure.

Radiants

Each item is placed on one of four concentric rings representing our recommended adoption stance:

Adopt

Technologies we have high confidence in. Proven in production with strong community support; we'd recommend them for appropriate use cases without hesitation.

Trial

Worth pursuing. These show strong promise and are mature enough to invest time in learning and piloting. Consider them for new projects where you can absorb some risk.

Assess

Worth exploring to understand how they might affect your organisation. Research is warranted, but they're not yet ready for serious investment.

Hold

Proceed with caution. Technologies that are immature, overhyped, or being superseded. Not recommended for new work, though existing usage may be fine.
